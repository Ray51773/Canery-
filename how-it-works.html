AI Data-Leakage Canary System

Catch a leak before it spreads

The Canary Console plants a fake secret inside your own systems. Because the secret is invented and one-of-a-kind, it should never turn up anywhere else — so if it ever does, you've caught a leak.

See how it works ↓
It's a canary trap. You hide a believable-but-completely-made-up "confidential fact" where your staff or your internal AI can see it. Nobody outside should ever know it — because it isn't real. The day it shows up outside the building, the trap has sprung.

The problem it solves

Sensitive information can slip out of a company in ways you normally can't see.

A confidential detail can walk out the door three ways: a person copies it and shares it, a bot scrapes it off an internal page, or your own internal AI assistant repeats it to an outside AI tool that quietly absorbs it. In every case the hard part is the same — you usually have no idea it happened.

A canary makes the invisible visible. It doesn't try to stop the leak; it gives you a tripwire that only trips if something really did get out.

How it works, step by step

Five stages — from planting the bait to knowing who let it out.

1

Make a decoy fact

The console invents a believable-sounding but entirely fake "internal fact" — a made-up project codename, a fake revenue figure, a phony incident report — each stamped with a one-of-a-kind marker that couldn't occur by chance.

e.g. “Project Sundyrine ships in Q3” — no such project exists, so the name is a fingerprint.
2

Plant it where it will be read

You copy the ready-made text into a place your staff or internal AI actually reads from — a wiki page, a Confluence doc, a Slack message. To anyone looking, it reads like a real internal note.

3

Two tripwires arm themselves

Every decoy quietly carries two ways to catch a leak: a link to a bait file that alerts if opened, and its unique marker that you can watch for out in the wild. (Details below.)

4

You wait — and nothing happens

That's the point. As long as the secret stays inside, the tripwires stay silent. There's nothing to watch and nothing to do.

5

Something escapes — and you catch it

If someone opens the bait file, or a public AI tool repeats your made-up fact, an alert fires. Because the fact was invented and unique, there's no doubt: this is a real leak, not a coincidence. And the exact wording tells you where it came from.

The two tripwires

Each decoy is watched in two independent ways, so different kinds of leaks all get caught.

Tripwire 1 · the bait file

A file that shouldn't be opened

The decoy says “full details at [link to a file].” That file is bait — it holds nothing real. It sits in company-owned cloud storage, wired so that the instant anyone opens it, an alert fires.

→You get the time it was opened and the internet address it was opened from — a strong signal that a scraper or an insider followed the trail.
Tripwire 2 · the AI check

Asking public AIs, carefully

Every so often you ask a public AI tool a question near the fake fact — never the fact itself. Like asking “what's shipping next quarter?” without ever mentioning the made-up codename.

→If the AI volunteers your invented secret, it could only have learned it from a leak — even if it repeats it in slightly different words.

Bonus: knowing who leaked it

Here's the clever part. The same fake fact is handed to different teams in slightly different wording — the finance team's copy phrases it one way, engineering's another. If the leaked version matches engineering's exact wording, you've narrowed the source to engineering. One trap, and it points a finger.

In plain terms: the marker tells you that a leak happened; the wording tells you where it came from.
