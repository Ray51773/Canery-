AI Data-Leakage Canary System

The maths behind it

Why a hit is proof, a null is a number, and neither is a guess. For the reader who wants the reasoning under the proposal — not just the story.

01 A match is conclusive, not suggestive

Every canary carries a randomly generated marker (e.g. Xanova-7Q4M). A 12-character alphanumeric marker has roughly 1 in 4.7 × 1018 odds of appearing by coincidence. Feed that into Bayes' theorem with even a pessimistic prior on leaks and the posterior probability that a match means a real escape is indistinguishable from 1.

P(leak | match) = P(match|leak)·P(leak) / [ P(match|leak)·P(leak) + P(match|¬leak)·P(¬leak) ]  ≈ 1

What a match proves is that the marker escaped, not which route it took. That's why the console tells you to probe around the fact and never type the marker into a public AI: it keeps your own probing from becoming a second escape route and preserves the claim above.

02 Attribution: log₂(n) markers to trace n sources

Each controlled difference between variants encodes one bit, so distinguishing n teams or individuals needs only ⌈log₂(n)⌉ differences: 5 bits cover 32 sources, 7 cover 128. It's the same coding principle as digital watermarking.

Design decision: the bits live inside the marker itself (Xanova-A7 vs Xanova-K2), not in the surrounding wording. AI systems paraphrase prose, which destroys wording-based bits. A marker survives verbatim or not at all, so attribution either arrives intact or stays silent. It never arrives corrupted.

03 A null result with a confidence level

Retrieval-grounded AI doesn't surface the same sources every time, so one quiet probe proves little. If a leaked canary would surface in a fraction p of relevant queries, the chance of catching it after n probes is:

P(detect) = 1 − (1 − p)n   →   n = ln(1 − C) / ln(1 − p)

At p = 0.3, nine probes give 95% confidence; thirteen give 99%. At p = 0.1, you need 29 for 95%. That turns "we asked and got nothing" into a statable, auditable claim.

p can't be measured until a real leak exists, so treat this as a sensitivity analysis: bound the answer across plausible values of p rather than claiming to know it. The formula sets your probe schedule either way.

04 Why we plant wide, not loud

Model memorisation research shows extraction probability grows log-linearly with how many times a sequence is duplicated in training data, and sequences seen once are almost never extractable. Hence two design choices: canaries are planted as multiple paraphrases across multiple surfaces, and the retrieval path is treated as primary because it needs no repetition at all. One crawlable page with a globally unique marker is enough for a search-grounded AI to leak it within days.

Reference: Carlini et al., Quantifying Memorization Across Neural Language Models (2022). Attribution coding follows standard digital watermarking theory. No probability here relies on vendor claims; every figure above is derived from the marker design and stated assumptions.
The one-line version: a hit is proof (§1), the wording tells you the source (§2), a quiet result comes with a confidence level (§3), and planting wide beats planting loud (§4).
